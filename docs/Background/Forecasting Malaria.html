Forecasting Malaria

Malaria studies have shown their association with climatic conditions such as rain patterns, temperature, and humidity[22][23]. In many places, the transfer is seasonal, with the highest number of times during and after the rainy season. Accurate forecasts and early warning signs of an increase in the disease burden can provide the necessary information on the use of targeted malaria control and prevention strategies that make the best use of limited resources. The malaria early warning system (MEWS) is considered a promising tool for preventing/reducing the burden of malaria by calculating the severity of the unexpectedly changing malaria-climate problems. MEWS priorities should include

  • case studies (early detection of epidemics)
  • preliminary weather-based warnings
  • annual weather forecasts.

Climate Change and Malaria‚Äã

Climate change is now a constant crisis for the whole world. Any prolonged variation in the mean state of the climate can be referred to as climate change[24]. Since 1950, global temperature has constantly risen at an unprecedented rate, causing global warming, resulting in irregular rain patterns and typical scenarios of drought, famine, and other such misfortunes. Few elementary models have suggested that this will cause an increase in malaria cases globally[25]. Because of the complex relationship between malaria and climate change, there will be an increase in malaria transmission in areas where malaria is common, where the disease is under control, and where there has been no malaria as the climate changes[26]. Increased temperatures, rainfall, and humidity may increase the number of malaria-carrying mosquitoes in the highlands, resulting in an increase in malaria transmission in areas not previously reported. In low-lying areas where malaria is already a problem, warmer temperatures will alter the parasite‚Äôs growth cycle for mosquitoes to grow faster, increasing the infection and thus contributing to the burden of the disease.

Seasonality and Non-Linearity in Malaria Forecasting‚Äã

In zoonotic diseases like Malaria, seasonality plays an important role between vector and host. Vectors and their hosts are subject to climate-related and climate-independent seasonal variations (e.g., temperature) and climate-independent (e.g., day-length). Seasonal variations affect the abundance and demographic processes of both vectors and hosts. Vectors can be affected by how temperature can change from season to season, resulting in their development, activity, and disease transmission role. The lifecycle and activity level of the host can be affected as well, affecting how fast infected or immune animals die and how fast uninfected animals are borne, with resultant impacts on the epidemiology of vector-borne zoonoses.

Vector seasonality due to temperature affects vectors‚Äô development and activity, affecting the transmission. Host demographic processes (reproduction, birth, and mortality rates) are affected directly by weather and indirectly by resource availability which will drive the VBD epidemiology. The relationship between vector transmission and host contingency is complex and gives a non-linear behavior. Rogers and Randolph have shown a quadratic relationship between temperature and vector mortality. A non-linear model with complex seasonality can be achieved using machine learning-based methods or artificial neural networks.

Previous approaches‚Äã

In recent decades, many kinds of statistics predicting the incidence of malaria in recent months have been made. Exponentially weighted moving average models, auto-regressive integrated moving average (ARIMA) with seasonal features, and seasonal combined frequency (SARIMA) models are used in historical demographic data to assess their ability to estimate the number of malaria cases a few months in advance[27]. This time-series modeling is only suitable for a stationary or seasonal process and not ideal for predicting non-seasonal data. Other integrated remote sensing methods have been used to achieve the goal. Xgboost and SVM-based methods have also been used, but they are only effective in higher dimensions[28]. Few Binomial models that only use statistical significance have been used in Nigeria to serve the purpose[29]. In Kenya, spatial-temporal methods employed Bayesian statistics to predict indicators of malaria transmission, such as entomological import rates[30]. The accuracy of the models varies and is difficult to interpret in the studies given the diversity of forecasting methods used, including how models are evaluated.

AI/ML based Methods‚Äã

In the previous approaches, we know that an underlying non-linear function is required to establish a relationship between malaria and other variables. In these methods, we fix our mathematical function and slightly tune parameters to get a good function that can approximate the data. Supervised machine learning is a good function approximator given the input data that can map an underlying function without specifying the function beforehand. Function approximation is a technique for estimating an unknown underlying function using historical or available observations from the domain. In function approximation, the exact function is still unknown.
