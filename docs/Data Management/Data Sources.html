Data Sources

The IMACS Malaria Prediction & Planning Toolkit combines multiple sources of complex and dynamic data, applies advanced modelling techniques, machine learning, and artificial intelligence, and produces simple decision support tools for malaria program managers to support malaria elimination. In advancing the discipline of climate-informed malaria prediction and planning and supporting its integration in malaria control programs worldwide, high-quality health and climate data (temperature, precipitation, humidity, etc.) are necessary.

We have multiple climate data partners, including India Meteorological Department (IMD), World Meteorological Organization (WMO), the Copernicus data platform by ESA, Bhuvan, and other data partners. Odisha government has signed an MoU with Malaria No More (MNM) India to provide malaria data for Malkangiri and Koraput, the two most malaria-affected districts of Odisha

Health Data‚Äã

Since the beginning of the 21st century, the WHO has published global estimates of malaria statistics. Following this, India established the National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme (NVBDC) to provide the country‚Äôs malaria data. Under this program, the Ministry of Health has acknowledged the need for high-quality malaria data and has started collecting data at the village level

Figure 2.1: IMACS File Upload Portal. Choose file lets the user choose a local system file, and submit button pushes the file to the server

With the help of an MoU signed between MNM and Govt. of Odisha, we get access to the malaria data at the sub-centre level in the Malkangir and Koraput districts of Odisha. The data is collected by the ground-level Asha workers. Each Asha worker covers a population of 1000 people on average. This data is then compiled at the sub-centres (SC). Each sub-centre reports the data to Community health centres (CHCs). There can be more than 1 CHC in a district. There are sectors and blocks between a CHC and District level hierarchy. The CHC level health officers send the data to the District level health officers. Data entry operators at the district office send the data to the IMACS via a portal that connects to our data warehouse on Azure. Portal can be accessed via - https://imacsuploadportal.azurewebsites.net/. Fig - 2.1 shows the snap of the file upload portal

We have access to m4 (Fortnightly report of cases from subcentre/ PHC/ district/ state) data of the Koraput district at the SC level from 2015 to April 2022 and 2012 to April 2022 data of the Malkangiri district. Data come in a predefined format (m4 format) set by the government in an excel file. There are many missing data points and merged columns with coloured cells for better visual representation. We use python and Alteryx to pre-process the data and follow the ETL pipelines in Microsoft Azure (discussed in the following sections) to get tabular data for further analytics.

Figure 2.2: M4 Format of data set by NVBDC.

In the above-described chain of data sharing, the pen and paper method is used to collect and compile the data. The data is then digitalized at the higher nodes and stored in an excel sheet. Using the Data portal, we receive these excel sheets. The pen and paperbased methods are very inefficient and time-consuming. It also requires a lot of manual work and is error-prone. Since the data compilation at the higher nodes is not automated, we receive the data at a significant time lag. To solve the above-mentioned problem, we have developed an Android-based mobile application, DISHA App. The app is developed in Java and uses the android SDK. This malaria surveillance app has been developed to collect the data directly from the Asha workers/ground level workers to the IMACS team. The app database has a parallel configuration of the azure data portal. This app will solve our two purposes -

  • To collect the data directly from the Asha workers/ground level workers to the IMACS team. This will ensure data quality and accuracy. This will also ensure data security.
  • To provide a platform where we can validate the data collected by the pen and paperbased method. Initially, we are expecting a gap/difference between the two data sources due to logistics and operational reasons. Over time, we will be able to ensure that the data reports are the same regardless of the method used. This will remove the manual pen and paperwork and the error-prone nature of the data collection

Data Types‚Äã

Using our data portal, we receive multiple forms of data at different temporal resolutions. Our highest temporal resolution of data is monthly. The least temporal resolution of data is yearly. We receive data at differnt heirarchies-

  • SC level - Sub-center level data is collected at the ground level.
  • CHC level - Community health center level data is collected at the sub-center level and then compiled
  • District level - District level data is collected at the CHC level and then compiled.

  13. Bi Pf - The number of people who have positive Pf

  17. Slide Pv - Slides for Pv cases
  18. Slide Pf - Slides for Pf cases
  19. Total Tests - The total number of tests performed

  24. sc positive - The number of sc positive cases

Climate Data‚Äã

We refer to climate or meteorological data as data that is used to specify a region‚Äôs climate and weather conditions, such as precipitation, temperature, wind speed, cloud cover, current weather, and humidity. We have different data partners for each of the climate variables. We will discuss each of them individually.

  • Rainfall - The most reliable data source for rainfall is the India Meteorological Department (IMD). The IMD publishes weekly rainfall data for the entire of India every year. The data is available on the IMD website. The spatial resolution of data is 0.5 degrees by 0.5 degrees. We need to have the shape maps of the region of interest to extract the data in tabular form. Shape maps are vector data storage formats for storing geographic features‚Äô location, shape, and attributes. Since the data is available yearly, any monthly rainfall calculation is impossible. So we use ERA5 data published by ESA. It is a reanalysis of the climate data from the last 30 years. The data is available on the European Space Agency (ESA) website. The spatial resolution of data is 0.5 degrees by 0.5 degrees. Since it is reanalysis data, it is inaccurate compared to the IMD data.

  • Temperature - We get our year basis temperature data from IMD. It is not precisely the temperature data but minimum and maximum temperature values. The data is available on the IMD website. The spatial resolution of data is 1 degree by 1 degree. We need to have the shape maps of the region of interest to extract the data in tabular form. It has the same problem as the rainfall data, which is having a yearly update. So for monthly updates, we use the 2m-temperature data from the ERA5 data published by ESA. The data is available on the European Space Agency (ESA) website. The spatial resolution of data is 0.1 degree by 0.1 degree, which is very high. 2m-temperature is the temperature at 2m above the surface. The value of 2m-temperature is then converted to the minimum and maximum temperature values to match the original variable pattern. The accuracy of this data is lesser than the IMD because it is reanalysis data rather than observed data.

  • Humidity - Humidity is one of the critical variables in mapping the complex dynamics of malaria seasonality. Humidity data is not available on the IMD website, but we can use (Tmax - Tmin) as the proxy of humidity or use the less mathematically generated reanalysis data from ERA5. We have chosen to use the ERA5 data for the humidity. The spatial resolution of data is 0.1 degrees by 0.1 degrees. The temporal resolution of downloaded data is monthly, the same as health data.

  • Flood data - Flood data is tough to get in the proper format. The source of flood data is Bhuvan. Bhuvan is an Indian web-based utility that allows users to explore a set of map-based content prepared by the Indian Space Research Organisation. At Bhuvan, we can explore the historical flood data, filtered by state and year. The data is also very up-to-date.

  • Vegetation and land cover data - These are two different data sets but have the same data source as the ERA5 portal. Vegetation cover defines the density of malarial mosquito habitats, while land cover data shows how the human population is distributed across the land. The spatial resolution of data is 0.1 degrees by 0.1 degrees. The temporal resolution of downloaded data is monthly, the same as health data.

  • Satellite Image Data - The use of satellite image data with image processing and image segmentation has been planned to monitor the malaria dynamics effectively. We can capture the water bodies and population density based on the housing density will truly give us a proxy of how malaria will spread. The models and data procurement will be done in the coming weeks.

Meta Data‚Äã

Metadata is data about the data or documentation about the information which is required by the users. In data warehousing, metadata is one of the essential aspects. It increases coordination, understanding, identification, and utilization of information assets. Metadata is used for building, maintaining, managing, and using data warehouses. Metadata allows users access to help understand the content and find data. Our metadata is not a robust model yet, but with the developments in DISHA through app and collaboration, we seek to improve and maintain a good data warehouse. The details about the metadata are given below -

  • Location - We have located the coordinates of each SC and CHC in the Malkangiri and Koraput districts. This helps us visualize the data over a map and also checks the accuracy of the data through index matching via the DISHA app.
  • Date and Time - The pen and paper method is not efficient in detecting the time and date of malaria incidences. With the help of our DISHA app, we can directly track the date and time of malaria incidence and later use that data to follow up with patients by ground-level workers. This will help understand the nature of outbreaks in the area if they happen.
  • Travel History - This critical data will be collected via the DISHA app with built-in forms for travellers or guests. This will enlighten us in understanding whether malaria has been locally borne or spread by travelling. With the date time in check, we can also monitor the patient‚Äôs future travel plans, and since we have a shared database, we can track the malarial movements through travel. We are motivated to add more metadata with the reference from our scientific advisory committee.
